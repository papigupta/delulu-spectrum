# Delulu Spectrum — Creative Direction

*A tool for measuring how delulu you are about yourself.*

This doc is a filter to check decisions against, not a brand bible. When something comes up — a screen, a colour, a feature — run it through here. If a new idea fights the doc, the idea has to win the argument.

## The eigenquestion

**Are you the size you think you are?**

How far is your self-image from how you actually land — and which way. That's the whole product in one question.

One test sits under every decision: **does this make someone feel measured, or judged?** Measured is the product. Judged is the failure.

## What it measures

It's a tool. You pick it up and measure how delulu you are about yourself. That's it.

Plainly: the gap between how you see yourself and how the people who actually know you see you. You rate yourself. They rate you. The distance between the two is the reading.

It does not measure your personality — not who you are. The moment it judges who you are, it's broken. It only checks whether your view of yourself matches the view from outside.

This is a real, studied thing, not a vibe. The gap between how people rate themselves and how others rate them is well documented, and the research says a *slightly* optimistic read of yourself is the healthy setting — not dead-accuracy. We keep one honest rule, because it's the whole point: you can't see your own blind spot. The one instrument you have for measuring yourself is the one that's off. That's why we use other people's eyes instead of a quiz.

## Voice

Take the measurement dead seriously. Never take ourselves seriously.

The model is the best kind of doctor — the one who tells you something hard, but with enough warmth and humour that you're at ease before you've registered the news. Funny isn't decoration here. It's how the truth gets in. A person braced for judgement can't hear anything; a person who's just laughed can.

So: dry, warm, funny, completely unbothered. Plain enough that anyone, any age, gets every word with no explanation — no jargon, no therapy-speak, no showing off. And never about who someone is, only about how far off they are and which way. We don't flatter, because that rebuilds the delusion we're here to pop. We don't wound, because that's just a different delusion. Honest, with a slight warm tilt — the same setting the tool tells you to aim for.

## The spectrum

One spectrum. Both ends are delusion — being too hard on yourself is exactly as off as believing your own hype. That symmetry is the whole insight. Protect it.

But the two directions get different names, because they're opposite problems that need opposite advice. "Delulu" is the positive side only — it already means *inflated*, and stretching it to cover the other side would confuse everyone. The negative side gets its own plain name.

Distance is just intensity — further out is a louder version of the same thing, not a new category. And the healthy spot is a zone, not a point: roughly neutral to slightly positive, and where exactly depends on the situation — lean optimistic when belief actually moves the outcome, lean neutral when reality doesn't care what you think.

## Look and feel

An instrument with a personality. Locked.

Instrument bones: precise, calibrated, the number as the hero, a layout that says "this is real, trust the reading." The personality lives in the warmth and the voice, not in decoration.

What it is not: a quiz. A quiz reads as a toy, nobody trusts a toy, and a reading you don't trust is one you ignore. So it has to feel like a real measuring device that happens to be charming — not a personality test with confetti.

The core visual is the spirit level. Not as decoration — it's exactly right. One line, a true centre, two directions, and a bubble that settles off-true. It's the plainest, calmest way to show "you're off by this much, this way," and because it's a real tool, you believe it.

## The reveal moment

This is most of the product. Get this right and the rest is plumbing.

The spirit level is the hero. One bubble, one true centre, and a healthy zone that sits slightly to the positive side of centre — because slightly optimistic is the good setting, and the instrument should show that honestly rather than pretend dead-centre is the goal.

The bubble stays neutral — no red, no green, no alarms. The tool reports; it doesn't panic at you. The number carries the verdict, with one plain line under it and nothing else competing.

Underneath: where the gap comes from, broken down by trait — your read next to your people's read. (Still to be designed.)

What it should feel like: a mirror you didn't know existed. Honest, quiet, clear. Not a celebration, not a funeral. We're not building a sacred moment — just an accurate, unbothered one.

## The rater's job

The whole thing only works if the people rating are honest — and the natural instinct runs the other way. Round everyone up, be nice, protect the friendship. That instinct is warm, and it quietly does harm: a flattering rating just hands your friend back the exact blind spot they came here to lose.

So we say it plainly to raters: the most accurate rating is the kindest one. Going soft isn't a favour. The gift isn't a good score — it's an honest read they can't get anywhere else, from someone who actually knows them. Our job is to make honesty feel like the kind move, not the harsh one: anonymity makes it safe, the framing makes it generous.

## Flow

Remove fear, not friction. People don't quit over an extra step — they quit when they're scared. There are two fears to kill.

The rater is scared their honest answer will hurt the person, or the friendship. Fix it with speed and anonymity, so being honest feels safe (see above). The subject is scared the result will be bad. The tone handles most of that before they ever see the number, and a little ceremony at the reveal pulls them through.

So spend the effort lowering fear at each step — not shaving steps for their own sake.

## The decision filter

For any call — copy, colour, layout, feature — ask, in order:

1. Does this make someone feel measured, or judged? Measured wins.
2. Does it flatter, wound, or reflect? Reflect wins.
3. Would someone trust this reading, or wave it off as a quiz? Trust wins.
4. Could anyone understand it instantly? If not, rewrite it.

## Open questions

- **Product name.** "Delulu Spectrum" leans on the positive side, even though the spectrum is even. Recommendation: keep it — it's the word that makes people click, and the tool corrects the lean the moment you use it. A neutral name would kill the fun, and the fun is what makes people receptive. Your call.
- **Naming the zones and poles.** A separate exercise — not in this doc.
